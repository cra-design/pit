Donations and gifts on a personal tax return

  • Who can claim
  • Gifts you can claim
  • How much you can claim
  • How to claim

How much you can claim

  • Tax year: 2024| Prior tax years: 2022 and earlier
  • Federal tax return: Line 34900
  • Provincial or territorial tax return (if applicable): Line 58969

You can claim a tax credit based on the eligible amount of your gift to a qualified donee.

Generally, you can claim part or all of the eligible amount of your gifts, up to the limit of 75% of your net income for the year. You may be able to increase this limit if you give capital property, including depreciable property.

On this page

  • Eligible amount of the gift
  • Federal and provincial or territorial rates for 2024
  • Carrying forward tax credits

Eligible amount of the gift

In most cases, the eligible amount of your gift is the amount shown on your charitable donation receipt.

The eligible amount of the gift is the fair market value of the gifted property that exceeds the amount of an advantage, if any as it relates to the gift.

The advantage is generally the total value of any property, service, compensation, use, or any other benefit that you are entitled to as partial consideration for, in gratitude for, or in any other way related to the gift. The advantage may be contingent or receivable in the future, either to you, or a person or partnership not dealing at arm's length with you.

Federal and provincial or territorial rates for 2024

Generally, you can claim part or all of the eligible amount of your gifts, up to the limit of 75% of your net income (line 23600) for the year.

Exceptions to the 75% limit

There are 2 exceptions to the 75% limit:

  • Gifts of capital property are limited to 100% of your net income
  • For the year a person dies and year prior, the limit is extended to 100% of the person’s net income

Refer to: Gifts with special considerations

You may also claim eligible amounts made in the previous 5 years (or previous 10 years for ecological gifts made after February 10, 2014) if they have not been claimed before.
